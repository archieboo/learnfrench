Why French Sometimes Feels Surprisingly Chinese

Co-wrote with AI

As a native Chinese speaker who learned English to a near-native level before studying French, I found myself noticing something unexpected: although French and Chinese are two radically different languages, they often seem to organize meaning in remarkably similar ways. I was even tempted to think that the people behind the two languages perhaps share a similar philosophy and way of thinking about the world.

This is not a claim that French is “closer” to Chinese linguistically. It is not. French belongs to the Romance family, while Chinese belongs to the Sino-Tibetan family. Rather, the similarity lies in what I can only describe as a shared philosophy of expression: both languages frequently extend a single concrete image into a rich network of related meanings. English certainly does this too, but it often seems to prefer another strategy—creating or favoring a different lexical item where French and Chinese are content to stretch an existing one.

Some of the parallels are striking.

1. Both French and Chinese often preserve the underlying image

For example, the word parenthèse—the very word that prompted me to write this article. Literally, it means “parentheses.” But French naturally extends the image to describe many different situations:

  • Petite parenthèse… — “A quick aside.”
  • Faire une parenthèse dans sa carrière. — “To take a break from one’s career.”
  • Une parenthèse estivale. — “A summer interlude.”
  • Une parenthèse enchantée. — “A magical escape.”

All of these meanings preserve the same mental picture: something temporarily set outside the normal flow before eventually returning to it.

Chinese often works the same way. Take the word 路 (“road”). Starting from a literal path, it radiates into 思路 (a line of reasoning), 出路 (a way forward in life), 门路 (useful connections), and 路子 (an approach to getting something done). The image of a path never disappears—it simply keeps expanding. English, by contrast, abandons the image and reaches for a different word each time: reasoning, prospects, connections, method. The metaphor becomes lexicalized instead of remaining visible—much as French keeps the parenthèse image alive where English switches to interlude, hiatus, break, escape, or digression. It’s as if English is an engineer while French and Chinese are painters.

2. Both languages are comfortable with broad semantic fields

Another similarity is their willingness to let one common word cover a surprisingly wide range of meanings. Take the French verb mettre. It appears everywhere:

  • mettre la table
  • mettre fin
  • mettre en valeur
  • mettre en œuvre
  • mettre entre parenthèses

At first this seems arbitrary. But all of these expressions share the same underlying idea: placing something into a new position or state.

Chinese speakers are accustomed to this kind of semantic flexibility. The verb 打 works the same way. Its core image is a hand carrying out an action, and from that single root it spreads everywhere:

  • 打电话 — to make a phone call
  • 打车 — to hail a taxi
  • 打水 — to fetch water
  • 打伞 — to hold up an umbrella
  • 打工 — to work a job
  • 打折 — to give a discount

None of these feel inconsistent, because each traces back to the same conceptual core. English, however, prefers a different verb every time—make, hail, fetch, hold, work, discount.

What makes this possible is context. When a French speaker hears mettre, or a Chinese speaker hears 打, the surrounding words immediately fix the intended meaning, so the word itself never has to encode every nuance on its own. English, by contrast, tends to build more of the distinction directly into the vocabulary, choosing a different verb altogether rather than leaning on context to sort the meanings out.

3. English tends to lexicalize distinctions

Perhaps the biggest difference lies in how the three languages handle nuance. English possesses an unusually large vocabulary, thanks in part to centuries of borrowing from French, Latin, Greek, and many other languages.

Consider the simple idea of beginning something.

English offers:

  • begin
  • start
  • commence
  • initiate
  • launch

Or consider emphasis:

  • emphasize
  • highlight
  • underscore
  • accentuate
  • foreground

Finding the exact word matters in every language; the difference is the philosophy of how you get there. A good English writer masterfully selects the perfect word for the precise nuance they wish to convey. French and Chinese more often let a familiar word stretch and trust the context to supply the nuance.

This is a difference of tendency, not quantity. It does not mean French or Chinese are objectively “more metaphorical”—English has extraordinarily polysemous words of its own, like run, set, and break. It is simply that French and Chinese more often invite the speaker to see relationships between meanings, where English reaches for another word.

What This Changes for Me in Learning French

This is what changes how I study French. Relying on dictionary definitions alone leaves me with lists of unrelated meanings to memorize. It is far more productive to ask a single question: what is the core image behind this word? Once I can picture that image, many seemingly unrelated meanings suddenly become predictable.

  • A parenthèse is no longer just punctuation—it is anything set temporarily outside the normal flow.
  • 打 is no longer just “to hit”—it is a hand setting some action in motion. (Years away from China and counting—I remain cautiously optimistic I’ll never have to relearn my own mother tongue.)
  • Mettre is no longer simply “to put”—it is placing something into a new state.

That way of thinking feels surprisingly familiar as a Chinese speaker. Perhaps that is why, despite the enormous grammatical distance between the two languages, French has so often felt unexpectedly intuitive—full of small ah-ha moments where a new word already felt close to home.
